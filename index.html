About

Can money buy happiness?

The 2026 Happy City ranking has just been released, and Copenhagen has once again secured first place. Looking at the rest of the list, it’s standing out how much Northern European cities dominate the top 20.


While it’s well known that Northern European countries are generally wealthier than much of the rest of Europe, we — three non-Danish students who moved here from Germany, Greece and Italy — started to question whether these cities are truly the happiest. This led us to a broader question, which became the title of our project: Can money buy happiness?

Scroll






To investigate this, we used datasets from Eurostat, which provided insights into both financial conditions and happiness-related indicators across European countries.

In the following table, we provide an overview of the data used in the analysis. We focused on 11 key figures that cover both financial and happiness related aspects:

Type Key Figure Explanation of the key figure
Financial GDP [PPS] Gross Domestic Product per capita across countries in Purchasing Power Standards (scale to equalize the currencies and price levels)
Mean Income [PPS] Mean income per country
Median Income [PPS] Median income per country
People at risk of poverty [%] Share of population earning below 60% of the national median income
Inability to keep home warm [%] Share of people unable to keep their home adequately warm
Happiness Population percentage of deaths due to mental disorders due to alcohol [%] Percentage of the population which died due to mental disorders due to alcohol
Population percentage of deaths due to intentional self-harm [%] Percentage of the population which died due to intentional self-harm
Family life satisfaction rating [0-10] How satisfied families are with their life, rated 0–10
Overall Satisfaction Rating [0-10] How satisfied people are with their life overall, rated 0–10
Self-perceived health [%] Percentage of people who rated their health "fair", "good" or "very good"
Trust in persons [0-10] How much people trust in others, rated 0–10

After this first insight, we now want to give you a better feeling for how the different variables are related to each other.



But first: what is a correlation?



Think of correlation as a way to describe how two variables move together. For example, if two variables are positively correlated, it means that when one increases, the other tends to increase as well. If they are negatively correlated, one goes up while the other goes down. And if there is no clear pattern, then the variables are likely not correlated.

In the following plots, you can see the relationship between financial indicators (on the y-axis) and mental indicators (on the x-axis). Each point represents one observation (e.g. one country in one year). The red line shows the overall trend of the relationship.



Interpreting the plots is quite straightforward:



• If the red line slopes upwards, the variables are positively correlated.

• If it slopes downwards, the correlation is negative.

• If the line is more or less flat, there is no clear linear relationship between the variables.

One important note: the data has been standardized before plotting. This means that the values shown in the graphs are not directly comparable to real-world units.

What really matters here is the direction and strength of the relationship shown by the trend line.

Gross Domestic Product

One can see a clear positive correlation between GDP and life satisfaction, overall satisfaction, and self-perceived health. The relationship between GDP and trust in persons appears to be slightly weaker. In contrast, the population percentages of deaths due to self-harm and alcohol show a slight negative correlation with GDP.

Mean Income

Mean income is weakly positively correlated with trust in persons and deaths due to alcohol. At the same time, it shows a strong positive correlation with life satisfaction, overall satisfaction, and self-perceived health. In contrast, mean income is slightly negatively correlated with deaths due to self-harm.

Median Income

A very similar pattern emerges as for mean income. It is weakly positively correlated with trust in persons and deaths due to alcohol, while showing a strong positive correlation with life satisfaction, overall satisfaction, and self-perceived health. Median income is slightly negatively correlated with deaths due to self-harm.

People at Risk of Poverty

There is a slight negative correlation between the share of people at risk of poverty and death percentages, as well as with self-perceived health. In addition, there is a strong negative correlation with life satisfaction and overall satisfaction. The only indicator showing a positive relationship is trust in persons, though very weakly.

Inability to Keep Home Warm

The inability to keep the home warm is slightly negatively correlated with death percentages as well as with self-perceived health. Additionally, this financial indicator is strongly negatively correlated with both life satisfaction and overall satisfaction.







After getting an initial sense of the correlations between financial and happiness-related attributes, we now want to dive a bit deeper into some of these factors. To do so, we analyzed the data by sex and across four age groups, excluding the 0–15 category to keep the focus on the working population.

In the following visualization, you’ll find financial key figures on the left and happiness indicators on the right. Each key figure is shown in three versions: one for males, one for females, and one for the total population. The x-axis displays European countries, while the y-axis shows the corresponding values.

The bar charts represent the overall values per sex and country, while the lines indicate the different age groups.

As you’ve probably noticed, this topic is complex in more than one way.

Some parts of the data suggest a clear relationship between financial factors and happiness, while others seem to challenge that idea. Of course, poverty can come with serious limitations, and financial stability can create a sense of security. At the same time, having a lot of wealth can also bring stress or the fear of losing it.

Data can only show general patterns and trends.

It doesn’t capture personal experiences, individual values, or what happiness truly means to each person. Factors like culture, background, and life situation play a huge role here and can’t be fully reflected in numbers.

We intentionally avoided interpreting the data too strongly.

We kept everything open on purpose. We believe everyone should form their own opinion. In the end, the answer is subjective — you can rely on the data or reflect on your own experiences.

What really matters is that you feel comfortable with your final answer to the question:

Can money buy happiness?
