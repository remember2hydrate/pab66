About

Can money buy happiness?

The capital of Denmark, Copenhagen, recently secured first place in the Top 10 ranking of the happiest cities in the world. Looking at this ranking, it becomes noticeable that seven out of the ten cities are located in Northern or Central Europe, regions where financial conditions are generally stronger compared to other parts of Europe.


Based on this observation, our group, consisting of three international students, aimed to explore whether this relationship between financial stability and happiness can also be observed across Europe as a whole, and how it behaves in countries that are not as financially strong. This led us to a broader question, which became the title of our project: Can money buy happiness?

After this first insight, we now want to give you a better feeling for how the different variables are related to each other.

But first: what is a correlation?

Think of correlation as a way to describe how two variables move together. For example, if two variables are positively correlated, it means that when one increases, the other tends to increase as well. If they are negatively correlated, one goes up while the other goes down. And if there is no clear pattern, then the variables are likely not correlated.

In the following plots, you can see the relationship between financial indicators (on the y-axis) and mental indicators (on the x-axis). Each point represents one observation (e.g. one country in one year). The red line shows the overall trend of the relationship.

Interpreting the plots is quite straightforward:

• If the red line slopes upwards, the variables are positively correlated.

• If it slopes downwards, the correlation is negative.

• If the line is more or less flat, there is no clear linear relationship between the variables.

One important note: the data has been standardized before plotting. This means that the absolute values in the graphs are not directly comparable to real-world units. What really matters here is the direction and strength of the relationship shown by the trend line.



Gross Domestic Product
In the plot above, one can see a clear positive correlation between GDP and life satisfaction, overall satisfaction, and self-perceived health. The relationship between GDP and trust in persons appears to be slightly weaker. In contrast, the population percentages of deaths due to self-harm and alcohol show a slight negative correlation with GDP.

Mean Income
The correlation plots indicate that mean income is weakly positively correlated with trust in persons and the population percentage of deaths due to alcohol. At the same time, it shows a strong positive correlation with life satisfaction, overall satisfaction, and self-perceived health. In contrast, mean income is slightly negatively correlated with the population percentage of deaths due to self-harm.

Median Income
For median income, a very similar pattern can be observed as for mean income. It is weakly positively correlated with trust in persons and the population percentage of deaths due to alcohol, while showing a strong positive correlation with life satisfaction, overall satisfaction, and self-perceived health. In contrast, median income is slightly negatively correlated with the population percentage of deaths due to self-harm.

People at risk of poverty
The visualizations show a slight negative correlation between the share of people at risk of poverty and the population percentages of deaths, as well as with self-perceived health. In addition, there is a strong negative correlation with life satisfaction and overall satisfaction. The only mental indicator that shows a positive relationship is trust in persons, although this correlation appears to be very weak.

Inability to keep home warm
The figure shows that the inability to keep the house warm is slightly negatively correlated with the population percentages of deaths as well as with self-perceived health. Additionally, this financial indicator is strongly negatively correlated with both life satisfaction and overall satisfaction.



The question is: what's the real story behind the decrease in prostitution in San Francisco?

What needs to be noted is that the decrease in prostitution-related incidents in San Francisco does not necessarily indicate a reduction in the actual prevalence of prostitution. The lower reported incidents may reflect changes in law enforcement priorities, reporting practices, or the displacement of prostitution to other areas or online platforms.

Beyond the decline

The decline in prostitution-related incidents in San Francisco should be read cautiously. The data strongly suggest that visible street prostitution changed in form and geography rather than simply disappearing. Gentrification, policing changes, legal reforms, and the rise of online solicitation all seem to have reshaped where and how prostitution-related activity appears in official records. In some districts, declining counts may reflect displacement, reduced street visibility, or altered enforcement priorities rather than a true disappearance of the activity itself. Taken together, the pattern is less a clean story of reduction and more a story of transformation.
